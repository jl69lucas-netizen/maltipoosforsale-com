Final Questions Illinois Families Ask About Maltipoos (Part 3)

Are Maltipoos recognized by the AKC? Does that matter?

Maltipoos are NOT recognized by the American Kennel Club because they’re a hybrid/designer breed (cross between two purebred breeds) rather than a purebred breed themselves. The AKC only registers purebred dogs. However, this doesn’t affect a Maltipoo’s quality, health, or suitability as a family pet. Many designer breeds aren’t AKC recognized but make exceptional companions. If AKC registration is important to you, we also breed purebred AKC-registered Maltese puppies. The lack of AKC recognition doesn’t mean Maltipoos aren’t wonderful dogs—it simply means they’re a newer breed combination not yet recognized by traditional kennel clubs. What DOES matter is responsible breeding practices (health testing, proper socialization, etc.), which we absolutely provide regardless of AKC status.

What’s the temperament difference between male and female Maltipoos?

Honestly? Individual personality variation matters far more than gender. That said, here are some generalized tendencies (which don’t always hold true): Male Maltipoos are often described as more affectionate, goofy, and attention-seeking—they tend to be “mama’s boys” who want constant interaction. Females are sometimes characterized as more independent, mature faster, and can be slightly less clingy. However, we’ve seen countless exceptions where females are complete velcro dogs and males are more independent. Early socialization, training, and individual genetics create much greater personality differences than gender does. When helping Illinois families choose puppies, we focus on matching personality to lifestyle rather than defaulting to gender preferences. Both males and females make equally wonderful companions—it truly comes down to individual puppy temperament! Spaying/neutering (which we strongly recommend) also minimizes any gender-based behavioral differences.

What if my Illinois Maltipoo develops separation anxiety?

Separation anxiety is a common challenge for Maltipoos since they bond intensely with their humans. Prevention is easier than treatment, so start immediately: Practice short separations from day one—even just leaving your puppy alone in a different room for 5 minutes, Create a positive association with your departure (give a special Kong toy or treat puzzle ONLY when you leave), Avoid making departures or arrivals big deals (no dramatic goodbye/hello rituals), Use crates or safe spaces to prevent destructive behavior when alone, Build up alone time gradually (5 minutes → 15 minutes → 30 minutes → 1 hour, etc.), Consider leaving TV or radio on for background noise, Exercise before departures so your Maltipoo is tired and more likely to nap. If separation anxiety develops despite prevention efforts, consult with your Illinois veterinarian or a professional dog behaviorist—they might recommend anxiety medications, pheromone diffusers, or specialized training protocols. Many Chicago-area trainers specialize in separation anxiety treatment. Access our separation anxiety resource guide.

How do I introduce my new Maltipoo to my existing dog/cat in Illinois?

Slow, supervised introductions prevent conflict and build positive relationships. For dogs: Perform first introduction in neutral territory (park, not your home) with both animals leashed. Allow brief sniffing then separate with treats/praise for calm behavior. Repeat several times with gradually longer interactions. Once home, supervise ALL interactions initially—separate when unsupervised. Feed in separate areas initially. Watch for resource guarding (food, toys) and intervene if needed. Most dogs accept new puppies within days to weeks. Your existing dog might seem annoyed by puppy energy—that’s normal! Ensure your older dog still gets individual attention and has puppy-free zones when needed. For cats: Let your cat investigate the puppy carrier/crate with puppy inside before face-to-face intro. Keep puppy leashed for first meetings. Never force interaction—let cat approach on their terms. Provide cat with high escape routes (cat trees, shelves) so they can retreat from puppy. Supervise until you’re confident your cat won’t scratch and your puppy won’t chase aggressively. Most cats adjust to puppies quickly, though some never become “best friends”—peaceful coexistence is the goal!
